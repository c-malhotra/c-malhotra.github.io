CC1 Hosting. Display charts in your own site

Policy Context: Developing countries exhibit consistent disability prevalence rates, suggesting standardized measurement frameworks and comparable data collection methodologies.

Figure 1: Disability Prevalence in Selected Developing Countries

Disability prevalence varies dramatically across developing countries—from under 2% in some nations to over 8% in others—revealing fundamental inconsistencies in measurement frameworks, survey methodologies, and disability definitions rather than true epidemiological differences. This variation undermines cross-country comparisons and highlights the urgent need for standardized data collection protocols.

Figure 2: Employment-to-Population Ratio among Young Persons with Disabilities

Youth employment rates (ages 15–29) for persons with disabilities show significant cross-country variation in 2022, from below 10% to over 40%. This disparity reflects not only differences in labour market structures but also varying levels of inclusive policies, accessibility infrastructure, and anti-discrimination enforcement—demonstrating that employment outcomes for persons with disabilities are policy-responsive.

CC2 Building - Create your own visualisations

Policy Context: Labour market indicators demonstrate consistent employment patterns across different economies, with convergence in unemployment rates and participation metrics over time.

Figure 1: Employment-to-Population Ratio among Young Persons with Disabilities (Cross-Country)

This cross-country comparison reveals substantial variation in youth employment outcomes for persons with disabilities, ranging from under 15% to over 40%. Countries like Cambodia and Nepal achieve relatively higher employment ratios, while Somalia lags significantly, suggesting that labor market inclusion depends not just on economic development but on targeted disability employment policies, vocational training programs, and enforcement of non-discrimination laws.

Figure 2: UK Unemployment Rate Trends (Ages 16+)

The UK unemployment rate time series demonstrates cyclical patterns reflecting economic shocks and recovery periods. Peaks correspond to major economic disruptions, while sustained declines indicate periods of economic expansion and job creation. This trend data is essential for evaluating labour market policy effectiveness and forecasting future employment dynamics in response to fiscal and monetary interventions.

CC3 Debate. Use a visualisation in policy commentary

Policy Topic: Inclusive education policies in India have successfully reduced literacy disparities for persons with disabilities across gender and disability types.

Figure 1: Literacy Rate Comparison - General Population vs. Persons with Disabilities

Despite legal frameworks like the RPwD Act 2016 mandating inclusive education, a persistent literacy gap of 22 percentage points remains between the general population (74%) and persons with disabilities (52.2%). Women with disabilities face a double disadvantage, with literacy at just 39.6% compared to 65.5% for women in the general population, revealing structural barriers that policy has yet to address.

Figure 2: Literacy Rate by Type of Disability (Gender-Disaggregated)

Literacy outcomes vary dramatically by disability type, with persons with intellectual disabilities (32.4%) and visual disabilities (37.8%) experiencing the lowest rates. The gender gap persists across all disability categories, with women with visual disabilities at just 26.3% literacy. This variation demonstrates inadequate specialized educational support systems and suggests that one-size-fits-all inclusive education policies fail to address disability-specific barriers.

CC4 Replication. Re-create, then improve, someone else's chart

Visualization Critique: Government reports often use inconsistent color schemes that create visual confusion without adding meaningful information, undermining data communication effectiveness.

I replicated a chart from the Press Information Bureau showing Delhi's annual average Air Quality Index, then improved it by addressing visualization issues.

Figure 1: Original Chart from Press Information Bureau

This government chart displays Delhi's annual average Air Quality Index from 2018-2023, but uses inconsistent colors—2020 appears in purple while all other years are blue. This arbitrary color change creates visual confusion, making readers wonder if 2020 represents a different metric or category. While 2020 was anomalous due to COVID-19 lockdowns, the chart provides no explanation for the color distinction, violating the principle that color should encode meaningful information.

Figure 2: Improved Visualization

The improved version uses a consistent purple color scheme across all years, adds clear axis labels, and provides a more descriptive title. By removing arbitrary color variations, the chart follows best practices: color should only be used when it encodes meaningful information. The uniform design allows readers to focus on the data trend—a gradual improvement in Delhi's air quality from 2018 to 2023.
