Are People with Disabilities in India Structurally Excluded?

Making Policy Implementation Gaps Visible Through Data

Project Aims

This project traces structural exclusion of India's 2.68 crore persons with disabilities across five critical dimensions: enumeration, democratic participation, policy implementation, legislative voice, and corporate employment. Despite Census 2011 identifying 26.8 million PWDs, subsequent systems systematically exclude this population from rights and opportunities.

The visualisations reveal cascading exclusion: electoral systems deny democratic participation (states with largest PWD populations provide <1% home voting access); UDID registration fails to reach Census-identified populations (states like West Bengal show only 10% coverage); parliamentary representation remains concentrated (75% of constituencies raised zero disability questions, with North region disproportionately vocal); and corporate India reports near-complete exclusion (44.7% of NSE companies claim zero disabled employees, overall rate 0.52%).

By mapping implementation gaps between Census enumeration (what we know exists) and actual inclusion (UDID coverage, electoral access, legislative voice, corporate hiring), the project exposes where 2.68 crore Indians are being structurally left behind despite legal frameworks, policy promises, and corporate disclosure requirements. The analysis supports evidence-based intervention by revealing precise points of exclusion across government and private sectors.

1 Census 2011: Disability Demographics

Interactive Dashboard: 26.8 Million Lives Across Multiple Dimensions

This interactive dashboard explores Census 2011 disability data across three dimensions. The National Overview shows India's 26.8 crore PWD population with a severe gender gap (789 females per 1000 males). State Patterns reveals geographic concentration, with northeastern and mountain states showing highest prevalence (Sikkim 2.98%, Odisha 2.96%) whilst populous states like UP and Maharashtra house the largest absolute numbers despite lower percentages. Disability Types breakdown demonstrates Census 2011's medical model bias: visual impairments dominate (48.5%) whilst many conditions recognised under RPwD Act 2016 remain uncounted.

2 Electoral Accessibility Crisis

Are States Making Voting Accessible for People with Disabilities?

This visualisation reveals a critical accessibility crisis: states with the largest PWD voter populations provide minimal home voting access. Uttar Pradesh, with 12.6 lakh PWD voters, enables only 0.96% to vote from home, whilst Kerala demonstrates that 17% accessibility is achievable. The scatter plot (left) shows the relationship between PWD voter population and home voting percentage, with the geographic distribution (right) highlighting regional disparities. States in the bottom-right quadrant—large PWD populations with low accessibility—represent millions of voters facing significant barriers to democratic participation.

3 The Disability Recognition Crisis: Census vs UDID Coverage

Implementation Gap: Where Policy Fails People

Implementation Gap: States below the diagonal line fall short of 100% UDID registration—revealing the gap between Census enumeration and actual policy implementation. West Bengal's 10.32% coverage despite 2M+ persons with disabilities versus Chandigarh's 86% coverage shows this isn't about resources but implementation capacity and political prioritisation.

Beyond Percentages: The Human Scale of Exclusion

Beyond Percentages: Uttar Pradesh has 2.5 million people with disabilities still without UDID registration—more than the entire disabled population of 25 other states combined. This highlights how percentage-based analysis masks the scale of exclusion in populous states, requiring resource allocation weighted by absolute numbers, not just coverage rates.

4 Parliamentary Advocacy Tracking

Who Speaks for People with Disabilities in Parliament?

Primary Research Note: This visualisation is based on original data collection conducted by the Politics and Disability Forum, systematically sourced from Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha parliamentary records (2024-2025). This project establishes India's first parliamentary accountability mechanism on disability—tracking how elected representatives engage with disability rights beyond tokenistic welfare schemes.

Why This Matters: India's 2.68 crore persons with disabilities remain largely invisible from decision-making corridors. When MPs consistently raise disability issues beyond narrow frames of reservation and welfare, they create space for evidence-based policymaking that reaches the last mile. This map makes visible who is speaking, who is silent, and which issues are ignored.

How to Use: Toggle between Lok Sabha (543 constituencies) and Rajya Sabha (36 states) views. Search for specific MPs or regions. Hover over any area to see MP details, party affiliation, question themes, and access full parliamentary documents.

5 Corporate Accountability

The Corporate Disability Employment Crisis

Primary Research: Analysis of 1,227 NSE-listed companies' BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting) disclosures for FY 2024-25. Data was systematically extracted by looping through XML files from NSE's corporate archive using Python. The complete extraction code can be found in this Colab notebook, and the final processed dataset is available here. Across 8.3 million employees in India's largest corporations, only 43,135 (0.52%) are persons with disabilities—ten times below the 2.2% population prevalence.

Why This Matters: 547 companies (44.7%) report ZERO disabled employees despite collective workforces exceeding 2 million people. This zero-employment scandal persists across all company sizes—small firms (<100 employees), medium companies (100-1,000), large corporations (1,000-10,000), and very large enterprises (>10,000) all show identical exclusion patterns. The stacked bars reveal that company size provides no advantage: 92.3% of companies (1,133 firms) remain below 1% disabled employment regardless of organisational capacity. This exposes corporate India's systemic exclusion of persons with disabilities from formal employment, with self-reported BRSR disclosures revealing—not concealing—the accountability crisis.

How to Use: Click any category in the legend (Zero Disabled, Below 1%, 1-2%, Above 2%) to filter and highlight that employment band across all company sizes. This interactive filtering reveals patterns—clicking "Zero Disabled" shows which size categories face most severe exclusion, whilst "Above 2%" highlights the rare exceptions. The colour gradient from dark purple (zero) to bright purple (above 2%) makes performance gaps immediately visible across the four size categories.

Key Findings & Policy Implications

Evidence-Based Findings

  1. Enumeration-Implementation Gap: Census 2011 identified 26.8 million PWDs, yet only 3.6 million hold UDID cards (13.5% coverage nationally). This represents systematic exclusion from formal policy frameworks despite legal recognition under the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act 2016.
  2. Electoral Accessibility Crisis: States with largest PWD populations provide minimal home voting access. Uttar Pradesh (12.6 lakh PWD voters) enables only 0.96% home voting, whilst Kerala's 17.07% demonstrates this is political choice, not technical impossibility. This affects democratic participation of 28+ million eligible voters.
  3. Parliamentary Accountability Deficit: 75% of constituencies (405/540) recorded zero disability-related parliamentary questions (July 2024–January 2025). This legislative silence directly correlates with stagnation on employment mandate enforcement (RPwD Act 4% requirement) and accessibility standards implementation.
  4. Corporate Exclusion at Scale: 44.7% of NSE-listed companies report zero disabled employees. Across 8.3 million workers in India's largest corporations, only 43,135 (0.52%) are PWDs—ten times below the 2.2% national prevalence and far below the RPwD Act 2016's 4% employment mandate. All company sizes show identical exclusion patterns regardless of capacity.

Strategic Policy Interventions

  1. Mandatory UDID Verification & Audits: Legislate mandatory audit mechanisms to link Census enumeration with UDID registration. States below 50% coverage should face compliance deadlines with capacity-building support. Link state health/social welfare ministry funding to UDID coverage targets.
  2. Electoral Accessibility Standards: Enforce minimum 15% home voting facility across all states by 2027. Make accessibility infrastructure a prerequisite for Election Commission funding. Publish quarterly state-level accessibility scorecards for public accountability.
  3. Parliamentary Disability Accountability: Establish standing parliamentary committee with quarterly public reporting on question volumes and policy outcomes. Track disability advocacy as performance metric linked to committee representation. Incentivise constituency-level disability needs assessments.
  4. Corporate Employment Enforcement: Mandate BRSR+ independent third-party audits for disability employment disclosures. Companies below 1% disabled employment face ESG downgrade and exclusion from government procurement (Section 29 of RPwD Act). Link corporate board diversity quotas to disability representation.
  5. Targeted Resource Reallocation: Redirect CSR spending toward employment-linked skill development in low-UDID states (West Bengal 10.3%, Jharkhand 17.2%, Bihar 19.8%) rather than dispersed welfare initiatives. Create dedicated disability employment missions with measurable hiring targets.

Data Challenges & Solutions

Challenge 1: Census-UDID Temporal & Categorical Mismatch
Census 2011 uses 7 disability categories whilst RPwD Act 2016 recognises 21 conditions. Census data is 14 years old; UDID registrations reflect 2024.
Solution: Used Census 2011 counts as baseline denominator for UDID coverage rates. This conservative approach likely underestimates true gaps since Census 2011 undercounts conditions now recognised under RPwD Act 2016. Chart 3a shows percentage coverage; Chart 3b displays absolute numbers to reveal scale disparities.

Challenge 2: Broken Boundary Files & GeoJSON Availability
Most publicly available GeoJSON files for parliamentary constituencies (543 seats) and updated state boundaries had broken geometries, invalid coordinates, or outdated delimitations.
Solution: After testing 15+ sources, identified working files from udit-001/india-maps-data (state boundaries) and 2024-maps-supplement (parliamentary constituencies). Validated by rendering test maps against Election Commission's official list.

Challenge 3: State Name Matching Between Census & UDID Systems
Inconsistent naming conventions across datasets (e.g., "Orissa" vs "Odisha," "Pondicherry" vs "Puducherry"). Telangana didn't exist in Census 2011 but appears separately in UDID.
Solution: Manually standardised all state names through iterative matching. For Telangana, applied Andhra Pradesh's 2011 prevalence rate (2.87%) to separated population. Manually verified all 28 states + 8 UTs matched correctly.

Challenge 4: Corporate BRSR Data Extraction
1,227 NSE-listed companies file BRSR reports as unstructured XML with inconsistent disability field naming (e.g., "Differently Abled," "Persons with Disabilities," "Disabled Employees").
Solution: Built automated XML parsing pipeline (code here) using Python to extract disability employment fields via keyword matching. Disambiguated true zeros from missing data.

Challenge 5: Parliamentary Data Primary Collection & Name Matching
Parliamentary questions lack centralised APIs. MP names inconsistent between Politics and Disability Forum's question database and PRS Legislative Research's constituency database.
Solution: Systematic review of all parliamentary questions (July 2024–January 2025) with manual theme coding. Manual name matching in Python. ~10-12 MPs (~2%) excluded due to unresolvable name variations; 98% match rate documented.

Challenge 6: Electoral Accessibility Data Opacity
Election Commission's PWD voter database provides state totals but home voting facility usage unpublished. RTI data showed gaps for 12 states in 2024 elections.
Solution: Merged ECI's PWD voter counts with RTI-obtained home voting data. Missing states marked "Data Unavailable" in Chart 2 tooltips. Calculated accessibility only for states with complete data (23/28 states + 8 UTs).
